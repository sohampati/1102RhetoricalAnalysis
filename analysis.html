Soham Pati: The Consequences of George W. Bush's Angry Rhetoric Post 9/11

Opinion by Soham Pati

10 minute read

*

Published 8:00 AM EST, Fri March 15, 2024

(CNN) - George W. Bush's Post 9/11 Speech and angry rhetoric created a united America in the face of terror and adversity.

This address, often remembered for its tone of resolve and determination, also divided the American people and alienated marginalized groups. In this analysis, we will explore how the use of angry rhetoric in Bush's post-9/11 speech affected its ability to accomplish and its purpose with the American audience.

President George W. Bush addresses the nation the night of the September 11th attacks

To first answer the question of how angry rhetoric aided Bush’s message, we must first define what angry rhetoric is and the avenues that angry rhetoric can be pursued through. Angry rhetoric refers to language or discourse that is characterized by strong emotion, often in the form of anger or outrage. It involves using forceful or aggressive language to express one's opinions, beliefs, or grievances. Angry rhetoric can be employed in various contexts. For example, ways that angry rhetoric is expressed in speech is through inflection, metaphoric language, tone, and hand gestures.


We must also define the purpose of Bush's speech. Bush's speech aimed to achieve several key objectives:

  1. C​​omfort and Resolve: President Bush sought to provide comfort to a nation reeling from the shock and horror of the terrorist attacks on the World Trade Center, the Pentagon, and the hijacked airplane that crashed in rural Pennsylvania. He wanted to assure citizens that the United States would recover from this devastating blow.
  2. Reassurance: The President aimed to instill a sense of resolve by assuring the American people that the perpetrators of these heinous acts would be brought to justice. He wanted to convey that the nation would stand strong and united in the face of adversity.
  3. Leadership and Unity: President Bush’s speech emphasized leadership during a time of crisis. He wanted to inspire unity and encourage citizens to come together, transcending divisions and differences. The Oval Office address served as a rallying point for the nation.

Bush's adept use of language in his speeches served to unify the nation by fostering inclusivity. He skillfully addressed Muslims as a peaceful community while also acknowledging the anger directed towards them, thereby creating a more cohesive narrative.

According to Kiara Alfonseca, Bush made it a priority to meet with Islamic leaders during this time of crisis. He listened to their concerns at the Islamic center in Washington D.C. on September 17, 2001 and attempted to prove cooperation was possible with Muslims during a time where Islamophobia was encouraged and propagated (Alfonseca). The action described showcases a significant display of leadership and empathy.

In a time marked by widespread Islamophobia, where fear and prejudice against Muslim people were being actively encouraged and spread, Bush chose to listen to the concerns of others and sought to demonstrate that cooperation with Muslim communities was not only possible but also necessary. This action indicates a deep understanding of the power dynamics at play and the importance of fostering inclusivity and understanding in society. By attempting to prove that cooperation with Muslim people was feasible, the president not only challenged prevailing stereotypes and biases but also actively worked towards building bridges between different communities.

Bush also used inflection as a tool to advance his message. This technique was helpful in that it emphasized certain parts of the speech over others. Discussing the terrorists, his pitch rose, showing that the United States will not back down in its anger. He states, “America was targeted for attack because we’re the brightest beacon for freedom and opportunity in the world, and no one will keep that light from shining.” In this sentence, his pitch starts out lower, conveying sadness, but after a pause, his pitch rises and becomes more confident when stating how no enemy of the United States will succeed.

The differing tones in the discussion of the tragedy and the terrorists reflect a complex emotional response and strategic stance by the United States. When discussing the tragedy and its impact on the victims, Bush’s tone becomes sadder and more subdued, conveying empathy and compassion towards those who suffered. This subdued tone indicates a sense of mourning and loss for the lives that were taken or irreversibly altered, fostering empathy among the audience and emphasizing the gravity of the situation. In contrast, when addressing the terrorists responsible for the tragedy, the speaker's pitch rises, conveying a sense of indignation and determination. This assertive tone symbolizes the United States' refusal to tolerate acts of terrorism and its commitment to pursuing justice and ensuring security. By juxtaposing these tones, the speaker communicates a message of strength, solidarity, and resilience, affirming the country's resolve in confronting adversity while honoring the victims and their families.


Bush used direct eye contact and strong hand gestures to show strength and send a message that the US will come for Al Qaeda and anyone who aids the group. The President looks directly at the camera throughout the speech. He also keeps open palms to emphasize points and has minimal fidgeting. The purpose of this was to exude confidence and speak directly to whoever is watching the video. When Bush addresses Al Qaeda and its allies, the camera zooms in ever so slightly to signify the anger that he and his fellow Americans feel towards these heinous acts.


Finally, Bush attempted to use unified language and took an “Us vs. Them” throughout the speech. Bush stated, "Freedom itself was attacked this morning by a faceless coward" and "America and our friends and allies join with all those who want peace and security in the world, and we stand together to win the war against terrorism." This speech had an unintended effect. Bush’s words led to divisiveness and alienation of certain groups. By framing the conflict as a binary struggle between the United States and its allies and the terrorists, there was a risk of oversimplifying the complexities of global terrorism. This led to rising anger against Muslims across the world. Known as the anger of devils, this divisive sort of anger is extremely strong and often rules out rationality and understanding of the other side. (Lonergan, 7:55). The emphasis on unity and solidarity in the fight against terrorism inadvertently alienated certain communities or nations that did not fully align with the United States' approach. By making it seem like there were only two sides and that unity against certain groups was the only option for the US, Americans felt forced to support Ameria in efforts to stem terrorism even though the US supported unethical efforts down the line. This exclusionary rhetoric contributed to feel tings of marginalization or injustice, further fueling divisive anger and hindering efforts towards building constructive relationships and partnerships.


Conclusion


In conclusion, while George W. Bush's post-9/11 speech was crafted with the intention of comforting and unifying a grieving nation, its heavy reliance on angry rhetoric ultimately exacerbated divisions and prolonged the War on Terror. Bush's strategic use of language, inflection, and gestures may have momentarily rallied support, but his divisive "Us vs. Them" narrative alienated marginalized groups and oversimplified the complexities of global terrorism. The unintended consequences of this rhetoric, including the perpetuation of Islamophobia and the erosion of ethical boundaries in combating terrorism, cannot be ignored. As we reflect on history, it's crucial to recognize the profound impact of rhetoric on shaping public perception and policy decisions, underscoring the imperative for leaders to employ inclusive and empathetic language in times of crisis rather than resorting to divisive anger.
